Project Poseidon

Studying the Impact of Disasters on Transportation Infrastructure

Get Started

Introduction and Motivation

The impact of extremely rare and almost completely unexpected events (called “Black Swans”) is often underestimated, and plans to mitigate or repair their damage upon public infrastructure are often outdated or even non-existent. In many instances where catastrophes cause unprecedented damage to lives and property, it is often realized after the fact that most decision-makers did not even consider the possibility of such an event happening. The impact of these events is magnified due to the completely unprepared nature of its victims. One case in point is the 2004 Indian Ocean Tsunami which had a death toll of almost 240,000. Previous such events in the same region were in 1883 and 1400 and these had catastrophic casualties as well. The vast majority of people as well as decision-makers in some heavily-hit regions did not even know that tsunamis were a thing, let alone advocate preparative measures for one.

Policy discussions, decision-making, regulations and preparedness measures against disasters often center around:

  • Individual buildings:  Building codes, strengthened materials, better structural design, evacuation drills
  • Intra-city first response:  Removing encroachments on critical roads, funding for local first responders.
  • Prediction:  Tsunami warning systems, better weather satellites, weather prediction models.

However, when the public recovery infrastructure of an entire city is put out of action, which is often the case with extremely powerful disasters, help has to arrive from outside. This cannot happen in volume if the city is disconnected from all inter-city transportation networks.

Our project makes a post-facto study of the impact that such disasters can have on road connectivity by building road network graphs from datasets and running Monte-Carlo simulations to measure the vulnerability of each city to being unreachable in the aftermath of a disaster.

Related Work

Wisetjindawat et. al. perform a thorough study of the aftermath of the 2011 East Japan Earthquake in terms of its impact on water, gas, electricity and roads. Their focus is on building logistical plans for distributing food, water and medicines, as well as supporting the civilian population in temporary shelters until recovery. They attempt to estimate how many trucks, drivers, the quantity of fuel and the number of operational hours required to restore normalcy in the aftermath of the disaster. They compare the existing response plan with their proposed plan which they obtain by setting up an optimization problem. However, their road network analysis is primarily deterministic in nature and their focus is on response planning rather than vulnerability estimation. This paper provides the main inspiration for much of the disaster and damage modeling that we do in our work.

Datasets

SimpleMaps US Cities Dataset

This provides a list of cities in the US along with their latitude, longitude and population. A more advanced paid version also provides demographic information, income distribution, etc. However, geocoordinage and the population is enough for our project.

simplemaps.com

California Road Network

This dataset was prepared by dividing the inter-city road network of California into segments that are upto 10 km long and creating a graph out of the result. It provides us the latitude and longitudes of the endpoints of each segment.

cs.utah.edu

Building the graph

We first had to merge the above two datasets and produce an inter-city road graph.
We also required three different facets or views of the graph for the rest of our system.

  • Tile View

    Our goal is to generate a tiled map of California along with the set of road segments each tile contains. We generate this view by first drawing uniformly dividing California into 1km x 1km tiles. After that, we create a representation of the set of all tiles and store:

    • The latitude and longitude of the center of the tile
    • A set of Node IDs and Edge IDs of the road segments located within the tile.

    An obvious, but clever optimization that we did was to not store any information about tiles that do not contain any roads, bringing down the number of tiles by 96%.

  • Segment View

    Here, we wish to construct a graph where the nodes are endpoints of road segments, and the edges are the segments themselves. The node attributes are Latitude, Longitude, Node ID while the edge attributes are Start node ID, end node ID and edge ID.

    This can be formed by just reading the California road network dataset without any major pre-processing steps.

  • Settlement View

    For this view, we wish to construct a graph representation of the inter-city transportation network. This requires merging information from the Settlement dataset with the road dataset.

    Each node v in this view contains:

    1. A unique id: This identifies the settlement.
    2. Access roads: A set of nodes from V_seg that are within some geographical radius R of v.
    3. Latitude and longitude of the settlement’s center.
    4. Other information such as city name, population etc.

    Each edge (u, v) in this view represents a road directly connecting u to v without going through any other settlement. As useful auxiliary information, we store the IDs of the road segments that make up this road.

    We construct this view by starting with the Segment view and following the below algorithm:

    1. Attach all segment nodes within a 2km radius of each city to that city.
    2. Recursively merge pairs of road nodes A and B if B has a degree <= 2. This eliminates irrelevant nodes and reduces the number of nodes from 18668 to 2276.
    3. For each settlement, find out whether there is a direct link to another settlement through the reduced set of road nodes. If so, the settlements are directly connected.

    We distributed the computation required across 5 machines and it took 3 hours to build the above graph. We obtained a graph with 1505 cities and 6522 direct roads between them.
